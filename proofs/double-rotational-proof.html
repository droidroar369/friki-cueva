Double rotacional identity through exterior derivative

The double rotational identity

$$\vec\nabla\times(\vec\nabla\times\vec F)= \vec\nabla(\vec\nabla\cdot \vec F)- \nabla^2\vec F$$

usually is prooved through an identity of the Levi-Civita symbol. That identity often is presented without a proof because it requires some techniques of Linear Algebra. In this article, we proof the identity through properties of exterior derivative.

Symbology

  • \(*F\) Hodge dual of tensor field \(F\)
  • \(\omega\wedge\tau\) is the exterior product of differential forms
  • \(dF\) Exterior derivative of k-form \(F\)
  • \( \iota_X F\) contraction of tensor F in direction of vector field \(\vec X\), i.e., \( \iota_X F= F(X,...)\)
  • \( \mathcal L_X F\) Lie derivative of tensor F in direction of vector field \(\vec X\)

The rotational is a vectorial operator that only applies in 3D vector fields, which we can write as follows

$$\vec F= F_x\hat x+ F_y\hat y+ F_z\hat z$$

where \(\hat x\), \(\hat y\) y \(\hat z\) are the canonical basis vector in cartesian coordinates.

Proof of the theorem

First, we identify the basis vector as basis covectors, because exterior derivative only applies on k-forms. The trivial way to change vectors into covectors in cartesian coordinates is

$$\hat x \mapsto dx$$ $$\hat y \mapsto dy$$ $$\hat z \mapsto dz$$

Thus, we change the vector field \(\vec F\) by its equivalent covector form (we use Einstein notation)

$$F= F_x\ dx+ F_y\ dy+ F_z\ dz= F_i\ dx^i$$

The rotational is the exterior derivative applied on 1-forms, but the result of apply ext. derivative over 1-forms is a 2-form, thus we have to convert the result of the derivative into a 1-form, preserving the components of covector. The way to convert 2-forms into 1-forms (in 3D spaces) preserving components is by means of the Hodge dual

$$*(dx\wedge dy) = dz$$ $$*(dx\wedge dz) = -dy$$ $$*(dy\wedge dz) = dx$$

Then, under Hodge duality, the rotational of F can be expresed as follows

$$\vec\nabla\times \vec F= *dF$$

Now, we apply 2 times the rotational

$$\vec\nabla\times(\vec\nabla\times\vec F)= *d(*dF)= *d( *(dF_i\wedge dx^i) )=$$

Because both \(dF_i\) and \(dx^i\) are 1-forms, we use the identity \(*(\alpha\wedge\beta)= \iota_{\hat\beta}(*\alpha)\) (see lemma 1), where \(\hat\beta\) is the vectorial equivalent to the 1-form \(\beta\) (remember, \(dx\) equivales to \(\hat x\), \(dy\) to \(\hat y\) and \(dz\) to \(\hat z\)). To simplify the notation, we use the partial-derivative version of a vector field: the basis vectors change to partial derivatives \(\hat x_i= \partial_i\). Then:

$$= *d\iota_{\partial_i}(*dF_i) =$$

We use the Cartan's magical formule to convert \(d\iota_X \omega= \mathcal L_X\omega -\iota_X d\omega\)

$$= *[\mathcal L_{\partial_i}(*dF_i) -\iota_{\partial_i}d(*dF_i)]=$$

If the vector field X within the Lie derivative has constant components (like in the case of the basis vectors \(X_i=\partial_i\)), then Hodge dual conmutes with the derivative (see lemma 2):

$$= *[*\mathcal L_{\partial_i}(dF_i) -\iota_{\partial_i}d(*dF_i)]=$$

By linearity of dual Hodge, we can distribute the minus sign. In the first term, we use that in three dimensions, \(**\omega= \omega\), and next, we use that exterior derivative always conmutes with Lie derivative

$$= **\mathcal L_{\partial_i}(dF_i) -*\iota_{\partial_i}d(*dF_i)= d\mathcal L_{\partial_i}(F_i) -*\iota_{\partial_i}d(*dF_i)=$$

Because \(F_i\) are scalars, Lie derivative is simply the partial derivative:

$$= d(\partial_i F^i) -*\iota_{\partial_i}d(*dF_i)=$$

In the first term appears naturally the divergence of F (remember that Einstein notation represents a sum). In the second one, we use that the \(F_i\) are scalars, and then the laplacian can be expresed as \(\nabla^2f= *d(*df)\)

$$= d(\vec\nabla\cdot\vec F) -*\iota_{\partial_i}(**d(*dF_i))= d(\vec\nabla\cdot\vec F) -*\iota_{\partial_i}(*\nabla^2F_i)=$$

The Hodge dual of a scalar is always a volume form, in 3D: \(*1=dx\wedge dy\wedge dz\). By linearity of dual Hodge and tensor contraction, we can conmute the scalar term \(\nabla^2F_i\)

$$= d(\vec\nabla\cdot\vec F) -(\nabla^2F_i) *\iota_{\partial_i}(dx\wedge dy\wedge dz)=$$

Finally, we use the identity \(*\iota_{\partial_i}(dV)= dx^i\) (see lemma 3) for a volume form \(dV=dx^1\wedge...\wedge dx^n\)

$$= d(\vec\nabla\cdot\vec F) -\nabla^2F_i\ dx^i$$

If we want the result as a vector, then we have to change the \(dx^i\) basis into vectors \(\hat x_i\). The vectorial version of the ext. derivative \(df\) of a scalar is the gradient \(\vec\nabla f\), thus

$$\therefore \vec\nabla\times(\vec\nabla\times\vec F)= \vec\nabla(\vec\nabla\cdot\vec F) -\nabla^2F_i\ \hat x^i = \vec\nabla(\vec\nabla\cdot\vec F) -\nabla^2\vec F$$

Q.E.D. \(\blacksquare\)

Lemma 1

Let be α y β 1-forms in \(\mathbb R^n\). Then

$$*(\alpha\wedge\beta)= \iota_{\hat\beta}(*\alpha)$$

where \(\hat\beta\) is the associated vector to β, i.e., if \(\beta= \beta_i dx^i\), then \(\hat\beta= \beta_i \hat x^i\).

Proof: due to linearity of Hodge dual, it suffices to prove the lemma in the basis covectors \(\alpha= dx^i\) and \(\beta= dx^j\) with \(i< j\). The dual Hodge is defined in basis k-forms such that

$$\alpha\wedge *\alpha = dV= dx^1\wedge ...\wedge dx^n$$

Then, the Hodge we are looking for is

$$*(\alpha\wedge\beta)= *(dx^i\wedge dx^j)= (-1)^{i+j-1}dx^1\wedge... dx^{i-1}\wedge dx^{i+1}\wedge...dx^{j-1}\wedge dx^{j+1}\wedge...dx^n$$

We see that the dual of \(dx^i\wedge dx^j\) is the product of all the \(dx^k\) except when k=i or k=j, and a \((-1)^{i+j-1}\) sign to preserve right-orientation of the volume form \(dV\). On the other hand, the dual of α is

$$*\alpha= *dx^i= (-1)^{i-1}dx^1\wedge... dx^{i-1}\wedge dx^{i+1}\wedge...dx^n$$

I.e., \(*\alpha\) equals the product of all the \(dx^k\) except k=i, together with a sign \((-1)^{i-1}\) to preserve orientation. The equivalent vector to \(\beta=dx^j\) is \(\hat\beta= \hat x_j\), then if we apply tensor contraction

$$\iota_{\hat\beta}(*\alpha)= (-1)^{i-1}(dx^1\wedge... dx^{i-1}\wedge dx^{i+1}\wedge...dx^n)[\hat x_j, ...]$$

In the last step, square brakets mean the vectorial entries of tensor, and due to contraction the vector \(\hat x_j\) is applied at first entry. By antisymmetry of k-forms, we can conmute the first entry to place it at j-th position

$$\Longrightarrow \iota_{\hat\beta}(*\alpha)= (-1)^{i-1}(-1)^{j-2}(dx^1\wedge... dx^{i-1}\wedge dx^{i+1}\wedge... \underbrace{dx^j}_{j\text{-th}} \wedge... dx^n)[..., \underbrace{\hat x_j}_{j\text{-th}}, ...]$$

The \((-1)^{j-2}=(-1)^j\) sign appears because we have to conmute j-2 times the first entry: j-1 times to place at j-th position, minus one entry due that there is not \(dx^i\) factor. Now, both the \(\hat x_j\) vector and the term \(dx^j\) are at j-th position, then we can apply directly \(dx^j(\hat x_j)\). By properties of differentials, \(dx^j(\hat x_j)=1\), thus

$$\Longrightarrow \iota_{\hat\beta}(*\alpha)= (-1)^{i+j-1}(dx^1\wedge... dx^{i-1}\wedge dx^{i+1}\wedge...dx^{j-1}\wedge dx^{j+1}\wedge... dx^n)[...] = *(dx^i\wedge dx^j)$$

This ends the proof for the case \(\alpha=dx^i\) and \(\beta=dx^j\). By linearity of Hodge dual and tensor contractions, it extends to the more general case of 1-forms. \(\blacksquare\)

Lemma 2

Let be X a vector field with constant coefficients respect to a coordinate basis, i.e., \(X=c^i \partial_i\). If \(\omega\) is a k-form in \(\mathbb R^n\), then

$$\mathcal L_X(*\omega)= *\mathcal L_X\omega$$

Proof: due to linearity of Hodge dual and Lie derivative, we prove the theorem only in the case of basis vectors \(X=\partial_j\):

$$\mathcal L_{\partial_j}(*\omega)= \mathcal L_{\partial_j}(*(\omega_I dx^I)) =\mathcal L_{\partial_j}(\omega_I *dx^I)$$

We use the notation \(dx^I\) instead of \(dx^{i_1}\wedge...\wedge dx^{i_k}\) to represent k-forms merely to simplify writing, same for coefficients \(\omega_I= \omega_{i_1...i_k}\) (we are still using Einstein's notation). By Leibnitz rule of product

$$ =(\mathcal L_{\partial_j}\omega_I) *dx^I+ \omega_I \mathcal L_{\partial_j}(*dx^I)= *(\mathcal L_{\partial_j}\omega_I\ dx^I)+ \omega_I \mathcal L_{\partial_j}(*dx^I)$$

In the first term we have used the fact that dual Hodge conmutes scalars. The second term is zero, because \(\mathcal L_{\partial_i}(dx^{j_1}\wedge...\wedge dx^{j_p})=0\) for every p-form. We prove this by induction over p. For \(p=1\), we have a single diferential and we can conmute derivatives:

$$\mathcal L_{\partial_i}(dx^j)= d\mathcal L_{\partial_i}x^j= d(\partial_i x^j)= d\delta_i^j= 0$$

The last was zero because Kronecker delta is always a constant (0 or 1). We supose true for p, then we'll prove for p+1:

$$\mathcal L_{\partial_i}(dx^{j_1}\wedge...\wedge dx^{j_p}\wedge dx^{j_{p+1}})= \mathcal L_{\partial_i}(dx^{j_1}\wedge...\wedge dx^{j_p})\wedge dx^{j_{p+1}} + dx^{j_1}\wedge...\wedge dx^{j_p}\wedge \mathcal L_{\partial_i}(dx^{j_{p+1}})$$ $$= 0\wedge dx^{j_{p+1}} + dx^{j_1}\wedge...\wedge dx^{j_p}\wedge 0 = 0$$

The first term is zero by induction hypothesis, and the second one is the case p=1. Therefore, \(\mathcal L_{\partial_i}(dx^{j_1}\wedge...\wedge dx^{j_p})=0\) for every p-form. Then, if we apply this last result:

$$\Longrightarrow \mathcal L_{\partial_j}(*dx^I)= \mathcal L_{\partial_j}((-1)^\sigma dx^{j_1}\wedge...\wedge dx^{j_{n-k}})= 0$$

Where the \(j_k\) indexes are such that \( \{j_1,...,j_{n-k}\}\cap \{i_1,...,i_k\} =\emptyset\), i.e., the \(j_k\) indexes are all distincts to the \(i_k\)'s. The \((-1)^\sigma\) sign is to preserve orientation and is irrelevant here. Similarly, we can prove that

$$\mathcal L_{\partial_j}(dx^I)= \mathcal L_{\partial_j}(dx^{i_1}\wedge...\wedge dx^{i_k})= 0$$

Returning to the original problem:

$$\Longrightarrow \mathcal L_{\partial_j}(*\omega)= *(\mathcal L_{\partial_j}\omega_I\ dx^I)+ \omega_I \mathcal L_{\partial_j}(*dx^I)= *(\mathcal L_{\partial_j}\omega_I\ dx^I)+ 0= *(\mathcal L_{\partial_j}\omega_I\ dx^I+ \omega_I\underbrace{\mathcal L_{\partial_j}(dx^I)}_0)$$ $$= *(\mathcal L_{\partial_j}(\omega_I\ dx^I))= *\mathcal L_{\partial_j}\omega$$

By linearity of Lie derivative respect to constant vector fields, we can complete the proof

$$\therefore \mathcal L_{c^j\partial_j}(*\omega)= c^j\mathcal L_{\partial_j}(*\omega)= *c^j\mathcal L_{\partial_j}\omega= *\mathcal L_{c^j\partial_j}\omega$$

\(\blacksquare\)

Lemma 3

Let be \(dV= dx^1\wedge...\wedge dx^n\) a volume form in \(\mathbb R^n\). Then

$$*\iota_{\partial_i}(dV)= (-1)^{n-1}dx^i$$

Proof: Apply definition of tensor contraction and then the Hodge dual

$$*\iota_{\partial_i}(dV)= *((dx^1\wedge...\wedge dx^n)[\partial_i,...])= $$

Conmute first entry i-1 times to place it at i-th position (remember that due to antisymmetry, each conmutation changes the sign)

$$= *((-1)^{i-1}(dx^1\wedge...\wedge \underbrace{dx^i}_{i\text{-th}}\wedge... dx^n)[..., \underbrace{\partial_i}_{i\text{-th}}, ...])= $$

Again, if an entry and a factor term are at same position, we can apply directly \(dx^i(\partial_i)=1\) and contract the tensor

$$= *((-1)^{i-1}(dx^1\wedge...dx^{i-1}\wedge dx^{i+1}\wedge... dx^n)[...])= $$

The tensor within the Hodge dual is the dual of \(dx^i\), and due that in n dimensions \(**\alpha=(-1)^{n-1}\alpha\) for 1-forms α

$$=**dx^i =(-1)^{n-1}dx^i$$

In particular, for n=3, \(*\iota_{\partial_i}(dV)= dx^i\). \(\blacksquare\)
